# FEMA NIMS/ICS Research Notes for SafeGuard EAP Integration

## Source: FEMA NIMS Fact Sheet for Private Sector Organizations (May 2021)
URL: https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/documents/fema_nims-private-sector-fact-sheet_05-2021.pdf

### Key Principles for Private Sector Organizations

**Why NIMS matters for private sector:**
- Private sector organizations play vital roles in incident management
- Adopting NIMS procedures and terminology helps organizations integrate into community incident management preparedness efforts
- Effective incident management can make the difference between advancing or damaging an organization's reputation
- A fundamental responsibility is to provide for the welfare of employees and help employees return to normal operations as quickly as possible following an incident

**NIMS Implementation Recommendations for Private Sector:**
1. Adopt NIMS with a valid legal authority — NIMS is your organization's system of choice
2. Designate and maintain a Point of Contact (POC) to serve as the principal coordinator for NIMS implementation
3. Engage with other NIMS POCs in your community (local EOC, first response organizations)
4. Keep your POC list current, accessible, and accurate
5. Contact your FEMA Regional NIMS Coordinator for help
6. Participate in exercises
7. Develop after-action reports to identify key NIMS principles
8. Promote staff training and credentialing

**Command and Coordination:**
NIMS Command and Coordination structures apply to four areas:
- Tactical activities: applying ICS-guided resources on the scene
- Incident support: conducted at EOCs through operational and strategic coordination, resource acquisition, information gathering, analysis, and sharing
- Strategic coordination
- Recovery coordination

**Resource Management:**
- Identify and inventory deployable incident resources within your organization
- Coordinate allocation of goods and services with other organizations
- Participate in mutual aid — Memorandums of Understanding (MOU) or Memorandums of Agreement (MOA) with other organizations and agencies prior to an incident

## ICS Core Roles (from FEMA ICS 300 and ICS Organizational Structure documents)

### Command Level (Simplified for Small-Mid Organizations)
| ICS Role | Simplified Workplace Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| Incident Commander | Site Lead / Incident Lead | Overall authority; directs all response activities; communicates with external responders |
| Deputy Incident Commander | Secondary Lead / Backup Lead | Assumes command if Site Lead is unavailable; assists with coordination |
| Safety Officer | Safety Monitor | Monitors conditions for responder safety; authority to halt unsafe operations |
| Liaison Officer | External Coordinator | Primary contact for arriving emergency services (police, fire, EMS) |
| Public Information Officer | Communications Lead | Internal communications; manages information flow to staff |

### Operations Level (Simplified)
| ICS Role | Simplified Workplace Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| Operations Section Chief | Floor/Area Coordinator | Directs tactical operations in their zone; reports to Site Lead |
| Evacuation Group Supervisor | Evacuation Coordinator | Leads evacuation procedures; confirms all staff are out |
| Accountability Officer | Accountability Coordinator | Takes roll call at assembly points; reports missing persons to Site Lead |
| First Aid/Medical | First Aid Coordinator | Provides initial medical assistance until EMS arrives |

## All-Hazards Approach (FEMA Principle)

The all-hazards approach means one EAP framework covers ALL emergency types:
1. **Active Threat / Hostile Event** — uses ACTD protocol (Assess, Commit, Take Action [Lockout/Lockdown | Escape | Defend], Debrief)
2. **Medical Emergency** — first aid response, EMS notification, accountability
3. **Fire Event** — evacuation, fire suppression (if safe), FD coordination
4. **Severe Weather** — shelter-in-place, weather monitoring, all-clear procedures
5. **Utility/Infrastructure Failure** — evacuation or shelter decision, utility notification
6. **Bomb Threat / Suspicious Package** — evacuation, law enforcement notification, do not touch

## ACTD Framework (Proprietary — Dave Miller / Pursuit Pathways)

**ACTD replaces Run/Hide/Fight throughout the entire platform.**

| Phase | Description | Sub-options |
|---|---|---|
| **Assess** | Situational awareness — recognize the threat, gather information, determine severity | — |
| **Commit** | Make a decisive commitment to act — do not freeze; assign the Site Lead role | — |
| **Take Action** | Execute one of three tactical responses based on threat assessment | Lockout/Lockdown · Escape · Defend |
| **Debrief** | Post-incident accountability, reporting, recovery, and after-action review | — |

### Take Action Sub-Options Defined
- **Lockout/Lockdown**: Secure the facility or area; deny entry to the threat; barricade if necessary; silence communications; await all-clear from law enforcement
- **Escape**: Evacuate the building or area via the safest available route; move away from the threat; do not stop to gather belongings; proceed to designated assembly point
- **Defend**: As a last resort when Lockout and Escape are not possible; use available resources to defend against the threat; commit fully to defense

## FEMA ICS Chain of Command Principles (Simplified)

1. **Unity of Command**: Every individual reports to only one supervisor during an incident
2. **Span of Control**: Each supervisor manages 3–7 individuals (ideal: 5)
3. **Incident Action Plan**: Even informal, every incident should have a communicated plan
4. **Accountability**: All personnel must be accounted for at all times during an incident
5. **Common Terminology**: Use plain language — no codes, no jargon during emergencies
6. **Integrated Communications**: Pre-establish communication methods before an incident

## Communication Protocols (NIMS-Aligned)

### Internal Communications During an Incident
- Use pre-designated communication channels (PA system, phone tree, radio, text alert)
- Designate a primary and backup communication method
- Use plain language — no codes
- Site Lead receives all status reports; communicates up to external responders

### External Communications (911 / Emergency Services)
- Designated Emergency Caller role (one person calls 911 — prevents duplicate/conflicting calls)
- Provide: location, nature of emergency, number of people involved, your name and callback number
- Do NOT hang up until dispatcher releases you
- Designate a Liaison at the main entrance to meet and brief arriving responders
- Provide responders with: building layout, number of occupants, location of threat, known injuries

### Post-Incident Communications
- Account for all personnel before releasing information
- Notify next of kin through proper channels
- Complete incident documentation within 24 hours
- Submit after-action report within 72 hours

## Staff Accountability Procedures (NIMS-Aligned)

1. **Pre-incident**: Maintain current employee roster with emergency contacts
2. **During incident**: Accountability Coordinator takes roll at assembly point using roster
3. **Report to Site Lead**: Number accounted for, number missing, last known location of missing
4. **Do not re-enter**: No staff re-enters the building until law enforcement/fire gives all-clear
5. **Post-incident**: Final accountability report submitted to Site Lead and HR within 24 hours

---

## Coordination with External Responders (NIMS-Aligned)

When emergency services arrive:
1. Liaison Officer meets responders at the designated entry point
2. Brief responders on: nature of incident, building layout, number of occupants, location of threat, known injuries, actions already taken
3. Transfer command to arriving Incident Commander (law enforcement or fire)
4. Site Lead becomes support role — do not interfere with responder operations
5. Provide responders with access to building systems (keys, access cards, floor plans)
6. Maintain accountability of all staff throughout responder operations

---

## Post-Incident Stabilization and Recovery (NIMS-Aligned)

1. **Immediate stabilization**: Secure the scene; provide medical aid; account for all personnel
2. **Damage assessment**: Document all damage, injuries, and losses
3. **Business continuity**: Activate continuity plan; identify critical functions to restore
4. **Incident documentation**: Complete ICS Form 201 (Incident Briefing) equivalent within 24 hours
5. **After-action review**: Conduct within 72 hours; identify what worked, what failed, improvements needed
6. **Employee support**: Provide Critical Incident Stress Debriefing (CISD) resources
7. **Regulatory reporting**: Report to OSHA, insurance, and other required agencies within required timeframes
